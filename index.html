1. An Introduction To MIC@Home

The Mobile Inpatient Care @ Home (MIC@Home) initiative, launched by the Ministry of Health (MOH) through the MOH Office for Healthcare Transformation (MOHT), aims to offer eligible patients the option of receiving inpatient-level care in their own homes rather than in a hospital ward. The program was designed to alleviate the growing strain on hospital bed capacity in Singapore, which is particularly relevant in light of the aging population and increasing healthcare demands.

Since its pilot in 2022, the program has proven successful in improving patient satisfaction, reducing hospital-acquired infections, and lowering the length of hospital stays, while still providing care equivalent to what patients would receive in a hospital setting. By 2023, over 2,000 patients had benefited from MIC@Home, saving around 9,000 hospital bed days (Singapore's Public Hospital Bed Crunch: Are Radical Solutions Needed?, 2023).

1.1. Moving Care To The Home

The MIC@Home model allows patients with a range of conditions, including skin infections, urinary tract infections, and congestive heart failure, to receive care in the comfort of their own homes (Figure 1). Patients in this program receive hospital-level care at home, including vital sign monitoring, intravenous treatments, and blood tests, delivered by a combination of teleconsultations and home visits by a dedicated team of doctors, nurses, pharmacists, and other healthcare professionals.

One of the primary benefits of MIC@Home is the remote monitoring of patient conditions. This allows healthcare teams to track patients' progress, and in cases of deterioration, emergency medical services can be activated, ensuring that the patient is provided immediate care, without the need for an inpatient stay. The initiative also allows patients to avoid the risks of hospital-acquired infections, further enhancing their recovery experience.

In line with the government's five-year plan to add 1,900 beds to the public healthcare system by 2026, MIC@Home serves as a critical tool in managing hospital bed shortages while maintaining high standards of care (DELIVERING CARE BEYOND HOSPITALS, 2024). The program is expected to increase the number of virtual beds to 300 by the end of 2024.

1.2. Key Stakeholders

The success of MIC@Home relies on the collaboration of several key stakeholders, each of whom plays a critical role in ensuring that patients receive the necessary care at home.

1.2.1. Hospital Department

The hospital department consists of doctors, nurses, therapists, and administrative staff who provide oversight and management of patient care. Their roles include:

  • Monitoring patient health through regular consultations and assessments.
  • Delivering care, including treatments such as intravenous medication, wound care, and other necessary procedures.
  • Educating and supporting patients on their conditions, treatment plans, and how to manage their care at home.
  • Responding to emergencies, either through home visits or activating emergency medical services if a patient's condition deteriorates.

1.2.2. Outsourced Medical Team

The outsourced medical team complements the hospital department by providing additional support tailored to the patient's needs. Their roles include:

  • Monitoring patient health remotely and in person, ensuring continuous care and early detection of any issues.
  • Administering care such as wound dressing, medication management, and other specialized treatments as required.
  • Filling in gaps in hospital coverage, with flexible scheduling that allows them to provide support on a case-by-case basis.
  • Responding to patient needs in emergencies, ensuring that appropriate actions are taken to maintain patient safety.

1.2.3. Patients and Caregivers

Patients and caregivers are at the heart of the MIC@Home program, ensuring effective care and monitoring at home. Their roles include:

  • Participating in daily consultations with clinicians and assisting in monitoring and recording vital signs.
  • Assisting with care procedures, such as medication administration and managing basic needs, under the guidance of healthcare professionals.
  • Communicating regularly with the medical team to provide updates on the patient's condition and receive guidance on next steps.
  • Acting as the first line of support in managing patient care at home, contributing to better recovery and reducing hospital readmissions.



2. Problem Identification

With no initial problem statement provided by MOHT, our task began by identifying gaps and challenges within the MIC@Home model. Through in-depth interviews with MIC@Home nurses and discussions with the MOHT team, we examined patient and nurse journeys to identify specific pain points, limitations, and unmet needs (Appendix 1). This section outlines these insights and the resulting problem statements that will drive our approach to enhancing home-based patient care.

2.1. Pain points of Patients

2.1.1. Patients' User Journey

To better understand the experiences of patients within the MIC@Home program, a user journey map was developed (Appendix 2).



In the Before Daily Visits phase, patients are responsible for managing their health independently. This involves monitoring vital signs such as blood pressure, temperature, heart rate and SPO2 using the medical devices provided by the hospital (Figure 3). They must then document these readings by writing them down and taking pictures of the notes, which are subsequently sent to the healthcare team via WhatsApp. Pain points in this phase include anxiety about recovery progress, as patients may be uncertain whether they are improving or if their health is stable. Additionally, there is uncertainty about the accuracy of vital sign measurements, as patients may not feel confident in using the devices correctly, leading to doubts about the reliability of their readings.



During daily visits, healthcare providers check in on the patient's condition and assess their progress. One common pain point here is forgetting the medication schedule; patients may struggle to remember when to take their medications, which could lead to missed doses.

After the healthcare provider leaves, patients continue to monitor their health independently. One of the main pain points in this phase is difficulty remembering the instructions provided by the healthcare team. Since patients have to rely on manual methods of logging data, the process can feel cumbersome, and there’s a risk of missing important steps in their care plan. Another pain point is physical or emotional discomfort during or after medical exams, which can make patients feel overwhelmed or stressed.

During the recovery process, patients often feel disconnected from the healthcare team once the daily visits end. A key pain point is the lack of clarity around diagnosis and care plans, which leads to uncertainty about the recovery process. Additionally, patients experience feelings of emotional isolation, especially when they are left to manage their health without daily interaction with a healthcare provider.

2.2. Pain points of Nurses

2.2.1. Nurses' User Journey

To gain a deeper understanding of the experiences of nurses within the MIC@Home program, a user journey map was created (Appendix 2).



The journey begins with the Before Daily Visits phase, where nurses prepare by reviewing patient records, checking for updates from other healthcare professionals, and ensuring they have the necessary equipment and medications for the visit. This phase is vital for setting up effective care, but nurses often face challenges such as managing large amounts of patient data and ensuring its accuracy. These issues can delay preparations and potentially hinder timely care delivery.

In the During Daily Visits phase, nurses assess patient conditions, administer treatments, and engage with patients and their families. Time constraints are a major issue in this phase, as nurses are expected to provide comprehensive care within limited timeframes. This pressure can make it difficult for nurses to fully understand a patient’s condition or address all of their concerns. The emotional demands of providing care, especially to patients who may feel isolated or anxious, add another layer of complexity.

Following the visit, nurses enter the Post-Visit Documentation phase, where they update patient records and adjust care plans based on their findings. The challenge here is the pressure to complete documentation quickly while managing other responsibilities. The sheer volume of paperwork can lead to missed details, which may affect the quality of patient care. Furthermore, the need to rely on informal communication channels, such as WhatsApp, to share information with other healthcare professionals increases the risk of miscommunication, which can have significant consequences on patient care. Nurses also report the additional burden of maintaining hardcopy records, which is time-consuming and prone to errors.

The Collaboration and Communication phase is essential as nurses must communicate with other healthcare team members to ensure seamless care. However, gaps in communication and incomplete handovers between teams or shifts can leave nurses with fragmented information, making it harder to make informed decisions and maintain continuity of care.

4. Concept generation

4.1. Design Statement

The project aims to develop a comprehensive solution for virtual patient monitoring that facilitates seamless communication between patients and the healthcare team. This will be achieved through a software system featuring modules tailored to meet the specific needs of patients, nurses, and hospital care teams. A key component of this solution is the integration of Large Language Models (LLMs) to power an advanced chatbot feature. This chatbot will enhance patient engagement by addressing secondary-level concerns in the absence of healthcare professionals, providing reminders for medication and vital signs monitoring, and generating concise summaries for nursing staff. This approach is designed to improve care coordination, streamline workflows, and ultimately enhance patient outcomes.

5. Concept Development

Taking into account the specific requirements for patients and nurses. We came up with a wireframe with the features for the patients’ app (Figure 14) and nurses’ app (Figure 15). A detailed explanation of the functionalities of the features can be found in the appendix.

5.1. Wireframes (Patients’ Application)



The patient module consists of three key features: care plan to routine reminders, personalized health reports, and an AI-powered chatbot. Each of these features enhances patient engagement and support throughout the recovery process.
Key Features:

  1. Care Plan to Routine Reminders
    This feature utilizes the patient's care plan to extract crucial information regarding medication schedules and vital sign monitoring. By analyzing prescribed medications and their dosages, the system generates timely reminders for patients, ensuring they know when to take their medications or check their vitals.
  2. Personalized Health Reports
    This feature generates comprehensive health reports based on the vital signs submitted by patients. These reports provide insights into health status, highlight trends and assist caregivers in monitoring patient progress.
  3. AI-Powered Chatbot
    The AI-powered chatbot is designed using Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) techniques to ensure responses are accurate and relevant based on a curated knowledge base. It enhances engagement by offering immediate assistance for secondary-level concerns of patients and facilitates communication of their concerns with the healthcare team.

5.2. Wireframe (Nurses’ Application)



The nurses’ module has two main features: Concise summary generation and easy documentation.
Key features:

  1. Concise summary generation
    This feature generates concise summaries for nurses, providing essential health details about the patients they will attend to throughout the day. The summaries include updates on patient progress from previous shifts with necessary tasks that need to be performed. Additionally, the system reports the captured interactions between patients and the chatbot, highlighting unresolved queries to ensure that nurses are informed and can address any concerns promptly. This streamlined communication enhances care coordination and supports nurses in delivering effective patient care.
  2. Easy documentation
    The speech-to-text feature streamlines the documentation process by allowing nurses to dictate information verbally while performing their nursing procedure, significantly reducing the time and effort required for manual entry. This feature facilitates easier and more efficient documentation, enabling healthcare providers to focus more on patient care rather than administrative tasks. Additionally, the auto-formatting functionality ensures that all necessary information is accurately recorded in a structured manner. This feature automatically organizes dictated content into predefined formats, minimizing errors and ensuring compliance with documentation standards. Together, these capabilities enhance the overall efficiency of record-keeping and improve the quality of patient data management.

5.3. Feedback from MOHT

Overall, the nurses from MOHT expressed a positive outlook on the concept of implementing a chatbot constrained by a knowledge base, recognizing its potential utility in patient care. They believe that an informative chatbot with a warm tone would provide reassurance to patients, enhancing their overall experience while recovering at home. Additionally, the nurses suggested incorporating features for monitoring other vital signs, including options for color selection and tracking changes over time. This enhancement would allow for more comprehensive health monitoring, further supporting patients on their recovery journey. Overall, the feedback highlights the importance of developing a user-friendly and supportive chatbot that meets both patient needs and healthcare provider expectations.

6.1.2. Decision For Frontend

In the end, the framework that we chose is Flutter for a few main reasons. Firstly, most of the team have no experience with traditional frontend languages like Javascript or HTML, which means that the edge that React Native has over Flutter in terms of familiarity with the language is non-existent in the context of our team. Thus, the ease of setting up a Flutter development environment and the gentler learning curve associated with Flutter is comparatively beneficial for the team. In addition to that, when the team starts scaling the application up and implementing more features, debugging and maintenance is much easier due to static analysis being possible with Dart.

6.2.3. Proposed Backend Architecture

Below is a graphical summary of what our proposed backend architecture will look like.



There are two servers, one customized for supporting the chatbot interface and the other supporting the rest of the requests from the frontend, with both running with Python. This architecture of having two servers is mainly to meet the requirements of the chatbot interface, which is different from the rest of the interfaces that require information from the server.

6.2.4. Chatbot Server

The chatbot server is using Langchain, a framework for incorporating LLMs and databases into application logic. This logic is supported with LangServe and FastAPI, which serves to accept client requests and send back the processed responses. Below is a graphical representation of the chatbot server architecture.



The server accepts API requests with FastAPI, then using the credentials of the user sent in the request to query the patient interaction database and care plan to retrieve the patient’s care plan and past conversations with the chatbot. With the care plan and patient prompt, the knowledge database can then be queried to retrieve the most relevant documents based on the patient’s prompt and medical condition. All of the queried knowledge is then added as the context together with the system prompt and patient’s prompt to query the LLM, in this case Groq API, to receive a response. The information used as context constrains the LLM’s response, making it use the documents to retrieve information for its response, greatly reducing hallucinations from the LLM. The system prompt instructs the chatbot to construct its response with the appropriate tone and mannerisms that are desired, which in our case is to act like a nurse.



In addition to sending back a response to the frontend chatbot interface, the chatbot server also processes and stores the patient’s prompt into databases. Notably, the patient’s raw prompt is stored in the patient interaction database together with the chatbot server’s generated response, and the patient prompt is passed through a LLM to extract and store the patient’s vitals data if present.

6.2.5. Flask Server

6.2.6. LLM Choices

There are many choices of LLMs to use today, with ChatGPT, Gemini, Ollama, and many others. There are many benchmarks to show the relative performance of LLMs as well, as seen by figure 21 (LLM Leaderboard 2024, n.d.).



Based on Figure 21, it can be seen that OpenAI’s ChatGPT is regarded as the best performing model for general applications and for reasoning, which suits our chatbot’s requirements well. Therefore, in the later iterations of the application, the backend is likely to use OpenAI’s GPT API for its LLM queries. However, as OpenAI’s APIs require money to be used, it is not advisable to use it to test the initial backend configuration. Hence, the team is using Groq API, a free API service with a selection of models like llama3, for initial stages of the application development.

6.2.7. Database Management System Choices

From the backend architecture, there are five databases that will be used to store information. As for what types of databases will be used, there are a specific few to be taken into consideration, namely relational databases like MySQL, document-based databases like MongoDB, and vector databases like ChromaDB.

Relational databases use a collection of tables with references to each other, allowing data retrieved from a table to be used as the key to query another. This compartmentalizes information in the tables and the relation between tables improves query timings even with many data entries. Thus, this style of database is suitable for user data such as the patient vitals and care plan.

Document-based databases envision collections of data as documents instead of tables, which is more intuitive for certain types of data to be stored (Document Database - NoSQL, n.d.). This means that related data is stored in similar documents, and querying the database does not require a structured query, hence this style of database also being termed NoSQL. This style of database is fit for our patient conversation data and nurse reports, which can be seen as more of a long document than snippets of information in a table.

Vector databases store data as vector embeddings of the data instead of the data itself. This is particularly useful in retrieval operations of data, allowing a query to retrieve data by similarity of embeddings (What Is a Vector Database & How Does It Work? Use Cases + Examples, 2023). This is particularly useful in the implementation of RAG as a prompt can be embedded to query the database and retrieve the documents most similar in context. Therefore, this style of database is the most suitable for our medical information database.

Appendix 3: List of Features (Patient Module)

There are three main features in the patient module. Namely, care plan to routine reminders, personalized health report and AI-powered chatbot.

  1. Feature 1- Care plan to routine reminders

    A care plan is a personalized document that outlines the specific health needs of individuals receiving care. It acts as a roadmap for healthcare providers, detailing the necessary interventions and strategies tailored to each patient's unique medical needs. Developed through comprehensive assessments by a doctor, care plans are regularly reviewed and updated to adapt to the changing needs of the patient. They encompass essential information on medical management, vital signs monitoring and patient allergies ensuring a coordinated and effective approach to patient care.

    Patients are able to get the routine reminders in the following ways:

    • Push notification
    • In app homepage notification section
    • During casual interaction with the AI-powered chatbot




    With the care plan, patient information and medical history provided by the hospital team, we can extract key essential entities, including vital signs to be monitored and the specific times for monitoring. Additionally, we will capture details such as the names of medications, their dosages, and administration schedules, along with the patient's specific medical conditions. This information will be systematically stored in a patient information database in the backend. This information will firstly, be used to display or send push notification to patients when the medication or vitals has to be recorded. Secondly, the information can facilitate efficient access and management of patient data when interfacing with the Large Language Model (LLM) through Groq API to send reminders to patients through the chatbot feature.



  2. Feature 2 - Personalized health report from vitals reported.

    The personalized health report feature enables the generation of comprehensive medical health reports based on the vital signs submitted by patients. As patients log their vitals, such as blood pressure, heart rate, temperature, SpO2 and blood glucose level, the system compiles this data into a user-friendly format that highlights trends and deviations from normal ranges. This report can be generated from the vitals recorded within the day to across a few days. This provides patients with insights into their health status. Regularly updated health reports foster proactive management of chronic conditions and encourage patient engagement in their own care.

  3. Feature 3 - AI powered Chatbot

    The AI-powered chatbot is designed using Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) techniques to ensure responses are accurate and relevant based on a curated knowledge base. This chatbot serves as an interactive support tool for patients, addressing common inquiries related to their recovery journey and providing guidance on managing their health effectively. By offering immediate assistance for secondary-level concerns, the chatbot enhances patient engagement and satisfaction. Additionally, it can facilitate routine check-ins, remind patients of upcoming appointments, and generate concise summaries for nursing staff, thereby streamlining communication within the healthcare team.

  4. Feature 4 - Concise summary generation

    While the chatbot is equipped to handle patient queries effectively, it is essential that the medical advice provided by the chatbot is communicated to the nurses. This ensures that nurses are informed about the concerns and inquiries raised by patients, even in the absence of a medical professional. To address this need, we propose a feature that delivers a concise summary to nurses regarding the queries resolved by the chatbot, as well as any outstanding questions that remain unresolved. This functionality will enable nurses to better understand their patients' concerns and provide more informed care.

  5. Feature 5- Easy documentation

    Recognizing that patients' homes may not provide an ideal environment for nurses to set up personal devices for documentation, we aim to alleviate this challenge by implementing a speech-to-text input with transcription functionality. This feature will enable nurses to efficiently perform documentation updates during patient visits, allowing them to focus more on patient care rather than the constraints of their documentation process. By streamlining the documentation workflow in this manner, we enhance the overall efficiency and effectiveness of home healthcare delivery.
